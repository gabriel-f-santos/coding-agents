# Talking to Users Without Biasing Them — The Mom Test + JTBD

The founder interview (interview-founder.md) maps *your* understanding. This file is about
talking to **potential users** — and getting truth, not politeness. It powers both the live
conversations and the generated instruments (research-instruments.md).

## The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)

Even your mom will lie to protect your feelings. Good questions are immune to that. Three rules:

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** Don't pitch. The moment you expose your ego,
   people protect you with compliments ("the pathos problem"). Keep your idea out of the early
   conversation.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future.** People are
   great historians of their own behavior and terrible predictors of it.
   - ✅ "Como você lida com isso hoje?" · "Me conta da última vez que isso aconteceu." · "Quanto
     você pagou pela última ferramenta dessa categoria?" · "Quanto tempo isso te custou?"
   - ❌ "Você usaria um app que…?" · "Você pagaria por isso?" · "Você acha que seria útil?"
3. **Talk less, listen more.** Aim for them talking ~80% of the time.

### The 3 kinds of bad data (discard them)
- **Compliments** — "Que ideia legal!" → worthless; redirect to facts.
- **Fluff** (hypotheticals/generics) — "Eu sempre…", "Eu normalmente…", "Eu usaria…" → anchor
  to a specific past instance instead.
- **Wishlists** — "Vocês deviam ter a feature Z" → dig for the underlying problem, don't take
  the feature at face value.

### Signals you're getting truth
Concrete past stories, real money/time already spent, emotion about the problem, introductions
to others, commitments (time, reputation, money). "Currency" = a real concession (a meeting,
an intro, a pre-order). Anything free-and-easy is fluff.

## Jobs To Be Done — the Switch Interview (Christensen / Moesta)

People don't buy products; they **hire** them to make progress in a circumstance. The switch
interview reconstructs the moment someone adopted (or dropped) a solution. ~10–15 interviews
reveal the dominant patterns.

### The Four Forces acting on every switch
- **Push** — what's wrong with the current situation (the struggling moment).
- **Pull** — the attraction of the new solution.
- **Anxiety** — fears about the new thing (will it work? switching cost?).
- **Habit** — inertia / attachment to the current way.

Progress happens when Push + Pull **outweigh** Anxiety + Habit. Your product wins by amplifying
push/pull and reducing anxiety/habit.

### The timeline (reconstruct it, don't generalize)
`First thought` (when they first realized they needed something) → `Passive looking` →
`Struggling moment` / triggering event → `Active looking` → `Decision / switch` → `First use`.
Walk them back through real dates and events: "Quando foi a primeira vez que pensou nisso? O que
aconteceu naquele dia?"

## Putting it together

Use Mom-Test phrasing to populate a JTBD timeline: every question targets a concrete past
moment, and you map what you hear onto the four forces and the timeline. The output of a good
session is a **struggling moment** you can quote and a set of forces you can design against —
not a list of requested features.
